TO LOAN OR NOT TO LOAN


Bill: I would like a loan please.

Banker: What for?

Bill: I’m kinda bored and so I thought I might go to college. The closest one is Ramen University and it’s VERRRRRY expensive but I hear it’s a real party college!

Banker: What do you plan on studying while at this other town>

Bill: Oh, I have no idea. Dad just said I needed to get out of the house and this is one way to do it.

Banker: What job do you have and how much money have you put aside for these education expenses?

Bill: Well, I’ve never actually had a job, unless you count doing the dishes occasionally for my parents. But they really didn’t pay much so I wasn’t able to save any. Besides, I don’t like doing dishes much!

Banker: What job do you plan to have while you’re at college.

Bill: Well, while I am studying I can’t be expected to hold down a job at the same time. Anyway, that would leave almost no time for partying.

Banker: Hmmm, you’re making this tough for me. Do you have any collateral to put up for the loan?

Bill: What’s collateral? Oh wait, let me google it. Let’s see, “running side by side”. Huh? What does that have to do with a loan?

Banker: Never mind. Do you think your parents would co-sign on the loan?

Bill: I don’t think so. Dad said he didn’t want to have anything to do with it but if I was going to college there are people that would give me a loan to do it.

Banker: Well son, I don’t think I can help you. You don’t meet any of the requirements that we typically look at for a loan. But I happen to know the financial administrator at Ramen U and I think he can help you.

Bill: Hmmm, why is it he can help me but you can’t?

Banker: Well he loans out other peoples’ money, doesn’t have to make a profit and doesn’t take any responsibility for whether you pay back the loan or not.

Bill: Ho, seems like my kinda guy!


And so begins Bills college journey at Ramen U. He will find that he CAN get a loan without underwriting, (no credit check, no qualifications needed at all), based solely on his desire to go to college and his parents income. It’s called a “needs based loan” but Bill doesn’t actually “need” to go to college, he “wants” to, maybe. At least he is responding to all those folks in his life that say, “you really need a college education to succeed in life”. And the depth of his desire and his plan for success for college will never actually be called into question, reviewed on a periodic basis or monitored in any way. Those administering the loans don’t really care too much because it is other peoples’ money they are handing out. The financial counselor at Ramen U will not really question Bills’ motives because Bill obviously wants to attend there and it means easy money for the school. Why would they turn that down? It’s also easy for Bill, way too easy. The counselor will practically fill out the paperwork for Bill. He’s not even sure what he wants to do in college, whether or not he can be successful or not, and the payback for the loan will be so far in the future, it almost doesn’t even exist in his mind. He can forget all his troubles, forget all his cares, and go to Ramen U. As J. Wellington Wimpy would say, “I will gladly pay you tomorrow for a hamburger today”.


A lot of discussion is happening regarding the forgiveness of student loans. It’s possible that we really should forgive these loans because I believe we never should have made the loans in the first place. I would rather like to propose that the very giving of the loans in the first place was a mistake.



Let’s look at some of the reasons often given for the Higher Education Act of 1965, which has morphed significantly since it’s inception:


  1. LBJ’s stated reason for the program was that “for the individual, education is the path to achievement and fulfillment”. And more people are earning degrees at college. In 1964 about 10% of eligible US citizens earned college degrees. As of 2019 that figure is about 30%. If just getting more people into college is the goal, this has worked.

  2. The private market is unlikely to provide students with loans at affordable terms, largely because the loans are not a good business decision. But of course the government can use other people’s money to make bad business decisions. The average default rate is currently about 28%-29%, compares to 4%-6% on average for home or auto loans. This default rate is similar to the sub-prime home loans in 2008 that destroyed the economy for a period of time.

  3. Students are usually better off with loans than without them, everything else being equal. Many do get college degrees they might not have without the government loans. Yes, the individual is better off if the degree is useful. We could also just throw money at them and they would be “better off” than they were before. That is not the real question. The real question is: do the people providing the funds get a benefit and is that benefit worth the cost. In business this question is answered by the business succeeding or failing. In government programs this question is NEVER answered.

  4. More college graduates = a better workforce and therefor a boosted economy. This could be true, but again at what cost? Businesses certainly like that the government is spending other people’s money on educating their workforce, but is this really where the money should come from?

  5. Society is better off. I assume this means that society is better off financially. However this is unclear. The money for the loans came from “society” and 28%-29% of them are not being paid back. There may be gains for individuals that positively affect society but it is very difficult to know if there is actual benefit. If the benefit was so great, wouldn’t the direct beneficiaries of the loans be able to pay them back?


NOTE: All of these same reasons could be used to justify government providing loans to small businesses. More small businesses is better for the economy, the private market would not provide business loans that have a 28% failure rate, the businesses themselves would be “better off” because of the easy money and more businesses = more jobs! (And by the way, did you realize that the federal government DOES provide federally backed small business loans through the SBA. But of course they often do it very discriminately, and yes, they are easier to get than a standard bank business loan).


Here are some reasons why the government should NOT be providing these loans:


  1. Students are over-borrowing and not paying the loans back in large numbers. The AVERAGE debt of recent graduates is around $32,000. Student loan debt is skyrocketing. This is happening because of the ease of and lack of requirements to get a loan. If the loan provided sufficient value to the borrower they would be able to pay it back.


  2. This program is costing much more than expected. When the government switched to “direct” student loans, the CBO predicted that they would save 9 cents for every dollar lent. Instead it has cost 8 cents for every dollar lent. A complete financial failure, as would be expected of a government program not based on real incentives.


  3. This easy money created an increased (and false) demand for college degrees. Colleges and universities expanded their academic programs in the arts and humanities to suit students not interested in math and sciences. This has led to many more degrees that have much smaller monetary value. Financially, it makes no sense to take out a $165,000 loan for a master’s degree that leads to a job where the average annual salary is $38,000—yet thousands of young people are making this choice.

  4. College cost have increased astronomically, in large part due to the easy availability of government backed loans. Colleges and universities respond to supply and demand incentives just like any other business or person. With the increased availability of easy money came an increased demand for degrees. This resulted in increased prices for a college education.


  5. It is not the federal governments business to provide money or loans for education. The constitution does not afford this duty to the federal government, no matter how beneficial it may or may not be. In fact they should have no responsibility or hand in education at all. This is one of the many, many, many areas the government has taken on that should be left to the many states.
