The Education Bubble: the failure of the financial system of education

Introduction

Americans of all persuasions agree that the financial state of our higher education is dire. Perusing social media will provide you with countless posts from its mass of angry supplicants. Parents continue to implore their children to attend college, even while they wince at the thought of the cost. Students continue to follow the guidance of their elders, only to gradually embitter themselves as they come to understand the financial hole that they’ve goose-stepped into. In many ways the quality of our education is degenerating as well, but while quality and cost are by no means unrelated, today I will be addressing why the bill is so high. We can complain about gender studies majors another time.

In order to understand the current situation surrounding student debt and the soaring tuition at American colleges, I would like us to consider another financial debacle that many in the States are intimately familiar with: the Housing Crash of 2008. Most people are similarly confused by the cause of this particular crash, and at the risk of biting off more than I can chew, I think comparing and contrasting these two “markets” might leave you with a better understanding of both than might be obtained one at a time. I would argue that, while different in many important ways, there is a chilling similarity between the situation surrounding the higher education market today, and the housing market in the years leading up to the ‘08 crash.

Over the course of this article, I’ll detail how these two crises are different, and how they’re frighteningly similar. Let’s start with the more familiar housing market and work our way towards student debt.

Housing

In 2008 the US economy ground to a resounding halt. An elaborate financial house of cards that had been under construction for some twenty years finally fell over, and brought the average American down with it. Homes were foreclosed. Employees were fired. Pension and retirement funds dried up, and by the time all was said and done, trillions of dollars had been vaporized in the chaos.

The origins of the Housing Bubble have been contentiously debated by many. Many blame some combination of the “Free Market” (storm clouds approaching) and “Deregulation” (spooky background music). Throw that in a pot with some “Evil Capitalists and Bankers” and you can crash the economy. As we’ll see, however, the housing crash was precipitated first and foremost by bad underlying mortgage loans, and these loans were incentivized by government policy, some federal and some local, in order to solve the “affordable housing crisis.”

As Dr. Thomas Sowell says, there are no solutions. There are only trade-offs. Making housing “affordable” to more people of lower incomes may sound benevolent, but you cannot wave a magic wand and make it so. Policy decisions are often made in this wand-waving fashion, but you do not evaluate policies based on their intentions, you evaluate them based on their effects. The following are what I believe to be the major policies that led to the housing crash.

First, artificially low interest rates, which stemmed from the chronically low Federal Funds Rate, allowed banks to offer mortgages at below what would have been the going rate in a free economy. This allowed lenders to offer lower monthly payments, which made it possible for more low-income earners to qualify for loans. Additionally, it allowed middle and high-income earners to afford bigger loans for larger houses than would have been possible under market conditions. Overall, this incentivized bigger and riskier mortgages to people at multiple economic levels, which drove up housing prices.

Second, federal and state lawmakers introduced legislation and applied political and economic pressure to lenders over the years to make home ownership easier to attain for low-income individuals through both punitive and remunerative measures. Banks were given quotas of low-income and minority borrowers to meet, and if they did not could be subject to discrimination lawsuits, have mergers and other business transactions denied,

Third, the government backed entities like Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac purchased enormous numbers of mortgages in order to free up cash for front-end lenders. This allowed financial firms to make immediate profits off low quality mortgages while continuing to produce more, keeping the gravy train rolling and focusing the liability for default in higher financial institutions with more government connections.

Lastly, this quagmire of perverse incentives was compounded by the higher-level mortgage-backed securities (MBSs) and further collateralized debt obligations (CDOs) which were developed as tradeable assets on the larger markets. Financial firms would come to bet more on these securities than the value of their underlying assets (the actual home mortgages) with the expectation that if the assets themselves failed, the government would step in and bail out the principal owner of the underlying mortgages. Namely, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, which were government agencies in all but name at the time. And unfortunately, these speculators were essentially correct.

When the cards on the bottom quivered and fell (the bad mortgages defaulted), the entire house of cards fell in an enormous heap. All of these bad loans owned by the banks and Fannie and Freddie, and there were so many bad loans, went to zero, and the many billions bet on them were flushed down the toilet. Thus went the largest economic disaster in living memory.

Schooling

How does this relate to student loans? You might consider education and homes vastly different investments, and you’d be right. Both require large sums to purchase, but one is collateralized and the other is not. If you default on your mortgage, the bank can always seize your house and recoup some or all of their initial investment. If, however, you default on your student loans, there is nothing tangible for the bank to seize. The loan is only as viable as the productive skills and experiences it provides to the student, who must guarantee it with a share of his presumably higher productivity into the future.

Legislation and Pressure

The Higher Education Act of 1965 (HEA) was enacted by congress in the eponymous year, and brought the federal government into the business of incentivizing people to attend college. They did this by instituting new programs to create conditional grants and scholarships directly to students and qualifying schools, and allowing private lenders to issue loans under “student-friendly terms.” The liability for these loans would be absorbed by the government, thereby assuming the debt in case of default. The act has been reintroduced and greatly expanded numerous times, including in 1998 and 2015, increasing the funding and adding new programs to further incentivize collegiate involvement, especially focusing on underprivileged minority groups.

This may be a purely cynical calculation on the part of many, but I’ll assume the best of those involved and say that most of these politicians simply believe that college is a blanket good, no matter the cost. Unfortunately, this approach ignores the realities of higher education. Again, no solutions, only trade-offs. In an abstract sense, going to college in and of itself does not imbue one with the power to succeed. In lowering the barrier for entry by making money for school so easy to come by, all people are incentivized to take advantage of the program. In the end, just like the housing market, you end up with the education version of bad mortgages, namely lowered educational standards and nearly useless degrees.

If you ask a freshman why they’re going to college and/or what degree they intend to obtain, they’ll almost inevitably say,” I have no idea, I’ll figure that out while I’m in college.” All of the federal and state pressure over the years to include as many students as possible no matter their qualifications or aspirations has served to bloat the number of new degree programs and the administrative cost it requires to maintain them.

If college is an investment in your future, shouldn’t it be treated like an investment? You don’t often hear someone who wants to open a business take out a $30,000 loan on a delivery truck and then after signing the paperwork say aloud, “Maybe in a few years I’ll have figured out what I can do with this.” In much the same way that giving a mortgage to someone with poor credit is likely to lead to default, giving out blank checks to aimless children, most of whom have no experience managing money or debt, is a recipe for low skilled graduates prone to early default and long-term debt slavery.

Interest Rate Controls

During the lead-up to the housing crisis, the FED gradually ratcheted interest rates down below market rates (at some points literally below 0% when accounting for inflation) in order to stimulate the buying of houses. This led to the lending of so many subprime loans that the entire market collapsed under their weight; a not so classic example breaking the link between supply and demand by the destruction of the price mechanism.

As more and more of the student loan market has been absorbed by the government, an ever-larger portion of these loans have terms dictated not by market forces, but by politicians and bureaucrats. These politicians are incentivized to make the loans available to the widest number of people at the lowest possible rate they can in order to gain voting support, throwing money into all the open hands they can find. This means creating programs that make loans at lower than market rates, and then portraying higher education favorably so that the highest number of possible voters will utilize the program they’re responsible for.

Currently most federally owned loans have interest rates between 4% and 7%, while private loans range wider, between 2% and 14%, but with a much higher average rate of 10.4% in 2019. It is reasonable to assume that the average federal loan interest rate is well below the market rate for the average student. Additionally, in an unprecedented move, the Trump administration froze federal loan repayment in March 2020 in step with the rest of the pandemic response. After the succession of the Biden administration, this program was continued indefinitely, resulting in de facto 0% loans (again, below 0% when accounting for inflation). Falsely low interest rates lead to higher demand for credit and increased overall prices as seen in the housing bubble, and student loans have and will continue to follow suit.

College administrators will not hesitate to increase prices when federal loans continue to support their largess. Since 1977 the average cost of college tuition has risen by a yearly 6.28%, nearly doubling the average CPI of 3.5% over that time. Through that period, the primary government support for college has been the guarantee and subsidy of private student loans. After the Clinton and George W. Bush presidencies, however, our next problem quickly claimed the “biggest subsidy” crown.

Government Ownership

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac owned trillions in mortgage bonds by the start of the ‘08 housing crisis. Their exposure was absolutely enormous, and had the government not stepped in to guarantee their debts, the results would have been cataclysmic for those invested in the housing market. While Fannie and Freddie were not technically part of the government, they were Government Sponsored Entities (GSEs), private businesses that were created and supported by congress. It was unlikely that the government would allow them to fail, which allowed all lenders and financial firms depending upon their purchasing mortgage debt to completely ignore the inherent risks of the terrible loans they were creating and selling.

Over time the HEA, and other legislation built upon it, has led to the government not only guaranteeing private loans, but actually issuing and holding student debt itself. In fact, as of 2021 55-60% of all students who attend higher education receive loans with an average debt at graduation of $26,000-$36,000, and 93% of all student loans are owned directly or subsidized by the federal government. This adds up to about $1.6 trillion in debt, of which the Biden administration is currently attempting to “forgive” 10,000$ or 20,000$ for each person with student loans, depending upon the student’s Pell grant status.

The idea that federal credit expansion for student loans causes increases in tuition is generally referred to as the Bennett Hypothesis. Many disregard it, but there is good research to support this idea. In 2017 the Federal Reserve issued a staff report of a study they conducted which indicated that for every dollar of subsidized student loans given to colleges, the cost of tuition increased by sixty cents. A smaller but significant effect was observed with unsubsidized loans as well. Other studies have found similar effects from Pell grants. In quarter 3 of 2022 there is 291.9$ billion in outstanding subsidized federal student debt. This means colleges around the US collectively will have increased the total cost of tuition by 175$ billion dollars simply due to the existence of these loans.

This, in combination with the freezing of interest accrual, is effectively a doubling down on the bad incentives of the last half century. Students will find loans even more attractive as the current federal trend indicates that they may not have to pay them in the end. Why consider economical majors or career paths other than college? Allocating time and money to inefficient degrees and schools will continue as cost is not a concern. Lenders, public and private, will continue to dole out trillions to schools. Why would they be more cautious when they’ll eventually be reimbursed by the taxpayer regardless of the quality of their loans? Schools will continue to increase tuition prices, hire more administrators, build ever larger recreational complexes, and create less and less practical departments and degrees. After all, the great mass of teenagers will be flushed with government funds to disperse where they wish, and they must find ways to make themselves attractive to this notoriously unerudite demographic. Why would the collegiate financial aid office advise a student to pick a more practical major, or at least save some money to make a down payment before starting school? Their job is to sign up as many students as possible.

Creative Financial Instruments

The MBSs and CDOs that exacerbated the scale of the crash in ‘08 have been the principal focus of those who laid the blame for the fiasco at the feet of capitalism. It has been the general view of most that at the very least, these financial instruments that were used to spread risk around ended up causing further destruction, though there are a number of dissenters to this opinion. Either way, bad loans were the primary underlying problem with these instruments. However, a similar situation has arisen with student loans as was seen with mortgages.

Student debt is being packaged into Student Loan Asset Backed Securities (SLABS). These SLABS are analogous to MBSs, yet exhibit some even worse features than their predecessor. Similar to MBS, SLABS consist of many student loans packaged together, shares of which are then sold to investors as lines of stable, fairly risk-free profit. A key difference between these two securities lies in their underlying asset. Mortgages are a collateralized form of debt. If you default on your mortgage, the bank can seize your home and recoup some of their losses. Student loans, however, are usually not collateralized. Education ideally, though often no longer in practice, leads to the development of human capital, but not to any physical asset that could be repossessed. In cases of default, investors shoulder the risk of much greater losses. These losses are again much more damaging to any higher-level securities they underlie.

Currently, Direct Federal loans, which are the majority of federal student loans owned by the Department of Education, are not allowed to be securitized unless refinanced as private debt. In this way, market exposure due to SLABS is limited to private student loans and FFELs. Federal Family Education Loans (FFELs)are those made by private investors, but guaranteed in case of default by the federal government. Private loans are around 136$ billion, and FFELs are around 214$ billion. The scale here is certainly smaller than the trillions involved in the mortgage securities market, however the underlying assets are similarly unsustainable as we’ve discussed. Additionally, since such a large portion of these securities are backed directly by the government instead of indirectly as with Fannie and Freddie in ‘08, the government has an even more pronounced conflict of interest. Companies that own these federally guaranteed loans will be unfairly advantaged over their competitors, and, as in all these situations, corruption and fraud will be rampant.

Conclusion

Most Americans seem to believe this cycle can be repeated ad infinitum. A similar thought was purveyed by the proponents of the policies that inspired the housing bubble. I am not proposing that the same type of economic crash will result from the education market as did from the housing market. The scale of the markets, the way the loans behave, the interest rates, and the level of government exposure are all somewhat different. However, the general trend through both is the enormous market distortions created by government policy. As was shown in the housing market, when the government finds a solution, what results will be a worsening of that problem or the creation of new, unforeseen problems.

There are no solutions. There are only trade-offs.
